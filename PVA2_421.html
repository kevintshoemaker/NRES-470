PVA for Yellowstone grizzly bears!

By the time we meet on Thursday, March 29 be sure that this model is running on your laptop (in InsightMaker) because we will be exploring the model more fully in class together, and use this PVA to do some management-relevant scenario testing.

Our goal is to better understand the future of the grizzly bear population in Yellowstone park, and the processes that may regulate this population!

Let’s walk together through the basic steps in building and running a PVA model:

Step 1: life history diagram

Based on the literature that we have read, we determine that there are four main stages in the grizzly bear life history: cub, yearling, subadult and adult! The adult stage is the only one that is reproductive!

What about population size? For any simulation we always need to initialize abundance!

From Schwartz et al 2006 we know that:

  1. Adult (reproductive) females represent approximately a quarter (25%) of the total population.
  2. Total abundance of adult females in the greater Yellowstone park area (solid squares plus the open triangles) was 5 to 10 in 1983 and 40 to 50 in 2001/2002

Let’s use the lower bound for initial (1983) abundances (all female!) to initialize our model:

Density-dependence
  1. Let’s implement a new process into our model- density-dependence!

We know that when the adult (female) population size was around 5, yearling survival was around 82%

We also know that when the adult (female) population size was around 40, yearling survival dropped to around 54%

What is the linear relationship between population size and yearling survival? When abundance increased by 35, yearling survival rate decreased from 82% to 0.54% – a total of 28%.

SLOPE: For each additional individual in the adult population, yearling survival decreased by 0.8 percent (\(=\frac{0.28}{35}\)).

INTERCEPT: This can be interpreted as the expected yearling survival when there are no adults in the population. When there were 5 adults, yearling survival was 81.7%. If there were 0 individuals in the adult population, yearling survival would be \(81.7 + 0.8\cdot 5\), which is 85.7%.

To convert back to a fraction, we divide this linear model by 100!

So the linear formula for computing yearling survival as a function of adult female abundance should be:

\(Surv_{yearling} = \frac{\left ( 85.7 - 0.8*N_{female} \right )}{100}\)

Let’s implement this density-dependent process in Insightmaker!

  • Create a [Link] between female abundance and yearling survival.

  • Open the equation editor for yearling survival, and input the equation above!

Run the model! What do you notice?

Q: Why is this type of model called density-dependent?

  1. For the density-independent model, what is the extinction risk if you changed yearling survival to its lower confidence limit in the above table (from 2002-2011)? Does the parameter uncertainty in the above table make a difference for the conservation outlook for this population??

[Bonus question!]. Given that local managers have decided that a viable grizzly bear population should have a risk of extinction of < 5% over 100 years, can you identify a minimum viable population size (MVP) for this population?

HELPFUL HINTS:

  • All parameters aside from yearling survival should be the same between the two scenarios (so that the presence/absence of the density-dependence process is the only difference between the scenarios). I suggest you use all parameters estimated for the period 2002 to 2011, just to be consistent!

  • Use the “sensitivity testing” option in the “Tools” menu to run multiple simulations (>100 replicates). You can assess extinction risk using the following formula:

\(\frac{extinctions}{replicates}\)

Where extinctions is the total number of replicates where the abundance declined to zero, and replicates is the total number of independent simulation replicates you ran. Similarly, you can assess the probability of failing to meet an important conervation criterion (e.g., population remaining above MVP) as the fraction of replicates that failed to meet the specified criterion.
